Tambour and Kerf Bending Cabinet

Completed: Nov 2024 – Mar 2025
Key Skills: Prototyping, Manufacturing, CAD Design, Project Planning

Project Overview

I never did woodworking before this project, but was always impressed by tambour door structures and kerf bending to smooth out corners in wood, so I pledged to design the cabinet I wanted and commit to prototyping it until I could make it perfectly.

I started in November with a bill of materials, rough project timeline, and CAD model. From CAD, I had to start prototyping to ensure that I could actually make the components of this project before putting down serious money.

Prototyping Notes

I ended up going through about 4-5 iterations of my kerf bending using heat, water, steam, and other forming methods to get the curve I wanted. Eventually I went back to basics with some rough geometry calculations, and with some very fine cuts, I was able to get a successful proof of concept together.

After that kerf bending, I found it easy to make a proof of concept for the tambour door, and confident in my abilities to make the subsections, I moved onto the project at large.

Design Process

Making this cabinet, my goal was to avoid any 3D CNC work. When I posted it online at the end, I wanted to ensure anyone could make it with some time and dedication. As a result, I used a hand router for all operations, taking care to cut out custom MDF jigs that I used to guide my cuts in a human-CNC type of way.

Tambour Construction

After milling the two main slabs of the structure, I used a laser cutter and CAD to cut out all of the hexagons for the top section before moving onto the tambour door.

Of the whole project, the tambour door was the most labor intensive. I ended up making 80 separate slabs, numbered to preserve the flow of the grain, before gluing and milling them with a router to make the door. I cannot express how happy I was when I finally got it on the track for the first time.

Final Assembly

After making the tambour door, I moved to add kerfs into my two ~8 foot walnut plywood sheets, joining them together with maple strips and wrapping them around the chamfered edges of my slabs before wood gluing them round form together.

After making the outside, I just added in the interior walls, aligned the tambour door on its track, then glued the whole thing shut.

This has probably been my favorite project so far, but I have another woodworking project coming soon that will finally get some use out of our studio CNC, stay tuned!
